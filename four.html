Business Operations

Financing, Operating and Investing are the three major activities in business operations. Financing deals with the initial startup of the business as well funding the business throughout its lifetime. When a business owner seeks a loan from a bank or SBA, issues stock to investors, or a combination of both, the owner becomes part of the capital market.

Investing has to do with buying tools for a business. Examples: a cash register, machinery, land.

Operating activity takes care of day-to-day operations of a business by selling/creating products, providing services, or both.

Business creates a enormous amount of data. Think of how many sales a single Walmart makes in a day!

Four fundamental financial documents represent this data

Four Statements

Integrated Financial Statements

Cash Flow Statement

Cash flow statement is the checkbook of the business. It covers a period of time vs. a snapshot (balance sheet)

Cash flow covers transactions from operations, financing, and investing

Cash is a liquid asset on the Balance Sheet A = L + E

Liquidity is a termed used to denote whether or not a business is generating cash from its assets or convert its assets into cash quickly

Cash is not profit. A business can generate a cash but not necessarily make a profit. Conversely, a profitable business doesn't necessarily have cash-flow.

When validating a business, most investors look at the cash-flow statement, rather than the income statement. This is to check if there is a healthy flow of money going into the business, then coming out of it.


Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet is a snapshot (instance in time). It measures the financial condition of a business with respect to its total assets and claims or rights to those assets. Note the balance statement does not change over time.

Most important Equation: Assets = Liabilities + Owner Equity

Balance sheet insolvency: Assets < Liabilities + Owner Equity Think of a house that you bought for $200k but now is worth $140k. Even if you sell all your assets you cannot payoff the liabilities.

Balance sheet insolvency is different than cash flow insolvency, in that a business may have assets, but cannot convert the assets quickly enough to pay bills (Also known as liquidity).


Income Statement

Income statement shows booked income and the costs and expenses associated with making that income.

Income statement DOES NOT show cash-flow. Cash flow is separate statement.


Earnings Statement

Retained Earnings Statement reports accumulated retained earnings at beginning of the year and the addition of any net income that will be added to that retained earnings.

Retained Earnings = Previous R.E. + Net Income - Dividends Paid

It also reports dividends issued to investors. Dividends are cash payments made out to shareholders and are an type of cash out-flow.

Three things can be determined by the Earnings statement:

1. Give some or all money (dividends) to investors

2. Save the money for inside the business

3. Combination of 1 and 2

Retained earning does not report what is done with the retained earnings or cash-flow. It just reports the earnings.
